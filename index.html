About the Book

Marcus Pruitt came to Heron's Bluff, Georgia for a story. He always does. A forensic chemist turned investigative journalist, he arrives on the Georgia coast to write about heirs' property — the quiet legal mechanism that has been stripping Black coastal families of their land for generations. A week's work. A few interviews. Some bourbon.

Then he finds the alderman dead at the base of a decommissioned lighthouse before sunrise. Everything about the scene is wrong — starting with a chemical smell that has no business being anywhere near salt water.

Marcus notices these things. He can't help it. It's the curse of a forensic mind in a town that has been keeping its secrets since before the Civil War.

Old families, old deeds, old money — and a woman named Celeste Vaughn Monroe who seems to know exactly where all of it is buried. Someone in this sun-soaked coastal town is counting on Marcus Pruitt to stay in his lane.

He won't. The man the killer never sees coming has arrived. And the tide is coming in.

The Author

Atlas Valley

Atlas Valley writes across worlds — and means that in more than one sense.

The debut novel, Heron's Bluff, announced what careful readers will immediately recognize: a forensic eye, an unhurried wit, and a sense of place so precise you can smell the spartina grass and the diesel from the shrimp boats. The Georgia coast lives in these pages the way it lives in memory — layered, warm, and deeply unwilling to give up its secrets without a fight.

But coastal noir is only the beginning. Atlas Valley is equally at home in the vast silence between stars — building worlds where spacetime bends at the right frequency, where two ordinary men follow an iron ladder into something that defies every map ever drawn, and the only way home is through.

There is also a third register: the wild. A world where the boundary between instinct and identity is paper-thin, where the most dangerous thing in the room has nothing to do with shadows, and where love — real, inconvenient, bone-deep love — turns out to be the most preternatural force of all. That series is coming. Soon.

Three series. Three worlds. One voice — the kind that notices what a room is doing before anyone opens their mouth, then finds exactly the right word for it.

The pen name, like the best maps, tells you where you are while reminding you there is still territory left to explore.

"Every constellation is a story someone told about the dark. The stars just held their positions long enough for the rest of us to catch up."

— Atlas Valley
